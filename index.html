The Long War

The first eight years of the Iraq War visualized

Key Facts

  • DISPLACEMENT: 2.8 million persons remain internally displaced or have fled the country
  • CIVILIAN DEATHS: There have been over 150,000 documented Iraqi civilian deaths
  • COST: The total cost of the war to America is estimated to be upwards of $2 trillion

The Iraq War (2003-2011), also known as Operation Iraqi Freedom or the Second Persian Gulf War, was a two-phase conflict which took place in Iraq.

The first phase, which occurred in early 2003, saw forces from the United States, Great Britain, and other international parties invade the country and engage in conflict with Iraqi military and paramilitary forces. The Iraqi forces were quickly defeated.

The second phase, occurring over a longer time period, took the form of a U.S.-led occupation of the country with opposition from a local Iraqi insurgency. As violence started to decline in 2007, the U.S. began to withdraw its forces, with a formal withdrawal occurring near the end of 2011.

The Rise of the Islamic State (ISIS)

“The brutality of terrorists in Syria and Iraq forces us to look into the heart of darkness.” President Obama

Today

The chaos within Iraq over the course of the war allowed for terrorist groups like ISIS to grow in strength and numbers. They continue to demonstrate their influence across areas of Iraq and Syria, and it appears that their presence will persist so long as instability reigns


Towards Elimination

While he acknowledged that ISIS is not an "existential threat" to the United States, President Obama has remained firm in his commitment to defeating ISIS in Syria and Iraq. He has exercised caution when considering the possbility of exercising renewed military force, but has not ruled out the possibility moving forward

The War on the US Homefront

Adjusted for population, the midwest and Vermont bore a disproportionate share of the casualties

  • 4,482 US Soldiers were killed in Iraq from 2003 to the end of 2011, when most US troops were withdrawn.
  • Unlike past wars, many servicemembers were called upon to deploy repeated times over the course of the war, with some serving three or four one-year tours.
  • Since the rise of ISIS, US troops have returned to Iraq but in much smaller numbers than at the height of the war. An additional 12 US servicemembers have been killed in Iraq since the official withdrawal of US forces in December 2011.
  • Today, less than 0.5 percent of the American population serves in the armed forces, leaving many Americans estranged from the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan—and the sacrifices they have entailed.
